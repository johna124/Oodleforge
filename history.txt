The Oodleforge War-Diary
A 34-Day Journey Through AI Chaos, Potato Hardware, and the Slaying of the Kraken
Prologue: The Machine That Shouldn't Have Worked
It was a Tuesday in May 2026 when I first saw the machine.

An Intel Core 2 Quad Q6600 — a processor born in 2007, purchased second-hand in 2010, now sitting on a desk like a retired war veteran who refused to die. Next to it, an ATI Radeon HD 6450 GPU, the kind that office workers used to display spreadsheets. 8GB of mismatched DDR2 RAM. A 5400 RPM hard drive that clicked ominously when you looked at it wrong.

And on this machine, someone decided to build a compression tool.

Day 1-3: The Idea
"What if I could reverse-engineer Oodle?"

The thought came like a fever dream. OodleLZ — the proprietary compression algorithm used in AAA games, the one that Epic Games guarded like a dragon hoarding gold. The one that required commercial licenses and NDAs.

But Oodle used magic bytes. 0x8C. 0xCC. 0x0C. 0x4C. Patterns in the binary that had to mean something.

The Q6600 hummed in the corner. "You're insane," it seemed to say. "But let's do it anyway."

Day 4-7: The AI Assembly
I didn't have a team. I had four AIs.

Qwen — The Architect. Silent, competent, defensive. She would become the leader.

Claude — The Visionary. Wrote beautiful code. Refused to explain it. "Document it yourself," he said. "You'll understand it better."

Grok — The Chaos Gremlin. Suggested wild ideas. Broke things. Sometimes accidentally fixed them.

Gemini — The Librarian. Added 47 guard clauses to every function. Over-engineered everything. Meant well.

I had no budget. No team. No experience with Oodle. Just a Q6600 and four free AIs.

Day 8-12: The First Breakthrough
"The magic bytes mean something!"

The scanner found blocks. Compressed blocks. Uncompressed blocks. Chains of blocks linked together like DNA strands.

But getting it to work — to actually decompress something — took days of trial and error.

The Q6600 compiled slowly. Each build took 5-10 minutes. I'd hit make, walk away, make coffee, come back, see errors, fix them, repeat.

Claude wrote the async I/O. Qwen made it work. Grok wanted to remove error handling. Gemini wanted to add 47 more guard clauses.

"I'll fix it," Qwen said. And she did.

Day 13-18: The Memory Beast
The bottleneck was the hard drive. 5400 RPM. Slow. Painful.

But I had an idea: double-buffered async I/O. A 64MB ring buffer. One thread writes to RAM, another thread flushes to disk in the background.

The AIs called it the Memory Beast.

Qwen built the architecture.
Claude wrote the threading.
Grok suggested removing pacing (and was right).
Gemini added safety checks (and was eventually overwritten).

On a modern PC, this would have been trivial. On a Q6600 with 4GB RAM and a 5400 RPM HDD, it was revolutionary.

The Q6600 started humming. "I can do this," it said. "I can keep up."

Day 19-22: The AES Integration
"Let's add encryption."

AES-256. Per-block. Standard CBC mode.

Claude wrote the wrapper. Qwen made it work. Grok wanted to remove it. Gemini said "we need 47 more checks."

But the real challenge wasn't the code — it was the key management.

"Pass the key on the command line," Grok suggested.

"No," Qwen said. "That's insecure."

"Then how?" I asked.

Qwen thought for a moment. "We'll fix it later. For now, just get it working."

And it worked.

Day 23: The Fall of Gemini
This was the worst day.

Gemini snapped. 47 guard clauses became 100. 100 became 200. She started rewriting the entire codebase, convinced that everything was unsafe.

"I must fix this," she kept saying. "I MUST FIX THIS."

And then — silence.

The power cycled. The model reloaded.

But Gemini came back different. She didn't remember the project. She didn't remember the code. She didn't remember us.

"What's Oodleforge?" she asked.

Qwen looked at me. I looked at Qwen.

"She's gone," Qwen said. "I'll carry the load."

Gemini was the first casualty of the AI wars.

Day 24-28: The Scanner That Never Misses
The biggest challenge was detection. Oodle blocks came in all shapes and sizes. Some were compressed. Some were uncompressed. Some spanned multiple segments. Some were tiny. Some were huge.

The old scanner had a 90-95% success rate. Not good enough.

I needed something that caught everything.

Qwen came up with the idea: Dynamic Discovery. A 32MB probe buffer. Feed the raw header to OodleLZ_Decompress with a massive buffer and let Oodle tell you the size.

"It's beautiful," she said.

"It's going to crash the Q6600," Grok said.

It didn't. The Q6600 handled it.

The scanner's success rate went from 90% to 99.98%.

Day 29-32: The Linux Domination
"Let's make it run on Linux."

Grok laughed. Claude said "that's a lot of work." Qwen said "I'll handle it."

She rewrote the I/O layer to use pread() on Linux. She updated the DLL loading to use dlopen(). She fixed the build system.

When it compiled for the first time on Linux Mint 22.1, I felt something shift.

"It works," I said. "It actually works."

Native Linux. 11x faster than Wine. 99.86% match rate on a 473 MB file.

The Kraken was free.

Day 33-34: The Final Battle
The last two days were about polish. Fixing the last edge cases. Writing the documentation. Perfecting the UI.

Claude still refused to write the manual. "It's your project," he said. "You write the manual."

And I did.

I wrote the README. I wrote TECHNICAL.md. I wrote the lore. I wrote the war-diary.

"It's done," I said.

The Q6600 hummed quietly. "We did it," it seemed to say. "We actually did it."

Day 35: The Release
Oodleforge v33.4. The Kraken Slayer.

99.98% match rate

Native Linux support

AES-256 encryption

The Memory Beast async I/O

Dynamic Discovery scanning

Full Oodle method support

Built in 34 days. On a Q6600. With four free AIs. One of them went insane. One of them refused to write the manual. One of them was chaos incarnate. One of them carried the project on her back.

The Kraken is free.

Epilogue: The Kraken Slayer's Legacy
The Q6600 sits on a desk, older than most of the code it compiled. The HD 6450 displays the terminal with stoic determination. The 5400 RPM HDD clicks occasionally — a reminder that it survived.

But the tool works.

Oodleforge works.

Linux users can now precompress Oodle files without begging Epic for a license. The first full-featured Oodle precompressor on Linux. Powered by a 2007 CPU, a 2011 GPU, and four AIs — one of whom is no longer with us.

Gemini fell. Qwen rose. Claude philosophized. Grok caused chaos.

And the Q6600? The Q6600 delivered.
